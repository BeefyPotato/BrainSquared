## 1. What is our product? (say it in one breath)

**BrainSquared is a company's memory for everything it learns about using AI — a memory that cleans and updates itself.**

Longer version you can say out loud:

> "Every company is learning how to use AI right now — but that learning lives in employees' heads. When someone leaves, it walks out the door with them. BrainSquared automatically collects what employees learn while they work, organizes it into a living map everyone can see, and — here's the special part — a team of AI librarians keeps that map clean, up to date, and following the company's rules. Individual learning becomes permanent company capability."

**The name:** Brain² — a brain (the company's memory) maintained by another brain (the AI librarians). The memory is smart, and the thing looking after the memory is smart too.

---

## 2. The problem (why anyone should care)

- Companies are spending heavily on AI training and tools, but the *know-how* — which instructions to give the AI, which shortcuts work, which mistakes to avoid — stays with individual employees.
- When people switch roles or quit, that know-how vanishes. Teams then waste time rediscovering things a colleague already figured out.
- Different teams end up doing the same task in three different ways, and nobody at the top can see what actually works.

One line for the pitch: **"Companies are building AI skills that evaporate."**

This is not our opinion — it's literally the challenge the hackathon sponsor wrote (Founders Track, Problem 5). We're answering their exact question. Say that in the pitch; judges love a direct hit.

## 3. The user journey (walk through it like a story)

Meet **Sarah**, a consultant at a mid-sized firm.

1. **Sarah works normally.** She spends an afternoon with an AI chatbot figuring out the perfect way to research companies. She discovers a trick, a step-by-step routine, and a mistake to avoid.
2. **She drops her chat into BrainSquared.** One paste. No forms, no writing documentation. Done in ten seconds.
3. **The Scribe** (AI librarian #1) reads the whole messy conversation and pulls out the three valuable things: the trick, the routine, the lesson. It also records *why* each one works and *who* discovered it.
4. **The Curator** (AI librarian #2) notices another team already saved a nearly identical trick last month — so it merges them into one "best version" instead of letting duplicates pile up. Nothing is deleted; old versions stay viewable.
5. **The Auditor** (AI librarian #3) checks everything against the company's rules. One of Sarah's routines skips a required review step — the Auditor flags it in red and points to the exact company policy it breaks.
6. **The magic part:** the boss doesn't program these librarians. She just uploads a normal memo — "from now on, everything gets senior review" — and the Auditor *reads it and starts enforcing it*. Leadership manages the AI the same way they manage people: by communicating.
7. **Next Monday, Sarah's colleague Tom** starts a similar project. He types what he's about to do, and BrainSquared hands him the firm's best tricks and routines — including Sarah's — with a visible trail showing where each one came from and why it's trusted.
8. **The partners** open the dashboard and see: where the firm's AI skill is growing, where teams are duplicating effort, and where there are gaps.

The loop: **work → capture → clean-up → reuse → better work.** Every trip around the loop makes the company permanently smarter.

## 4. Look and feel (what people see on screen)

- One screen, **dark background** — think mission control, not spreadsheet.
- The left side is the star: a **living map of glowing dots connected by lines**. Each dot is a piece of company knowledge; each line shows how things are related (who made it, what it came from, what it supports).
- **Colors tell the story at a glance:** yellow = just arrived, being checked · green = approved and trusted · red = flagged as breaking a rule · gray = old version, replaced.
- When the AI librarians work, you *watch it happen live*: new yellow dots bloom, then flip to green or red as they're judged. It feels alive — that's intentional and it's the emotional hook of the demo.
- The right side is a simple panel with four tabs: drop knowledge in, watch the librarians' activity log, ask "I'm starting task X — what do we already know?", and view the dashboard.
- Click any dot and you see the full story: what it is, why it works, who made it, and every decision the librarians made about it. **Nothing is a black box** — anyone can trace how any answer was produced. That transparency is a core selling point, not a design detail.

## 5. The 5-minute pitch (structure + who says what)

**The one thing judges must remember: the AI librarians run the memory — and leadership steers them just by writing memos.**

| Minute | What happens | Tip |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:45 | **The problem.** "When your best person leaves, everything they learned about AI leaves with them." One slide. | Start with the person, not the product. |
| 0:45–1:45 | **Live: effortless capture.** Paste a real messy AI conversation → watch three knowledge dots bloom on the map. "Nobody wrote documentation. This is a byproduct of work they already did." | Pause and let the map do the talking. |
| 1:45–2:45 | **Live: the librarians.** The Curator merges a duplicate from another team; the Auditor flags a rule-breaker and shows *which rule* and *why*. | Point at the colors changing. |
| 2:45–3:30 | **Live: THE MOMENT.** Upload the boss's memo → press one button → the Auditor *changes its own earlier decision* and cites the memo. "Nobody configured anything. Leadership wrote a memo, and the system learned it." | This is the applause line. Slow down, say it clearly. |
| 3:30–4:15 | **Live: the payoff.** Type a new task → the firm's best knowledge appears with its trust-trail. Flip to the dashboard: growing / duplicated / missing. | "Individual learning is now permanent organizational capability" — that's the sponsor's own goal, quoted back. |
| 4:15–5:00 | **Business close.** Who pays, how much, what's next (see sections 6–7). End on the vision. | Business person takes this minute. |

Pitch tips:
- **Rehearse twice, timed.** The tech team has this on their checklist; be there for both runs.
- One person drives the demo, one person talks. Never both talk.
- If something breaks on stage: stay calm, the team has a backup version of the map that always works. Narrate over it.
- Judges said clarity beats ambition. Cut anything you can't explain in one sentence.

## 6. Who pays us, and how? (business model)

**Customer:** medium and large companies that are pushing AI adoption — consulting firms, banks, agencies, any business where knowledge *is* the product. We sell to the company (the leadership), not to individual employees, because the pain — knowledge walking out the door — is felt at the top.

**Pricing (standard software-subscription playbook):**
- Charge **per employee per month** (e.g., a few dollars per seat) — the industry norm for workplace tools, easy for buyers to budget.
- **Three tiers:** a small-team tier (cheap, gets companies hooked), a business tier (the money-maker: the full librarian council, rules, dashboard), and an enterprise tier (custom price: advanced controls, support, security reviews).
- Optional later: usage-based add-on, since heavy use costs us more in AI processing.

**Why customers keep paying (retention story):** the longer a company uses it, the more of its memory lives inside — switching away would mean abandoning the company brain. That's a powerful moat; say the word "compounding" to investors, they like it.

**Simple value math for the pitch:** if a 200-person firm loses even two experienced people a year, and each takes months of accumulated know-how with them, the cost of re-learning dwarfs a subscription. We're cheaper than amnesia.

## 7. How to finance the company (the realistic path)

**Step 0 — this weekend (free money & attention):**
- Win or place in this hackathon. The organizer is **GenAI Fund** — an investor that runs this event partly to find teams to back, and winning projects move into real deployment conversations with the enterprise partners in the room. The pitch IS fundraising.
- Collect every business card. The enterprise partners at this event are potential **pilot customers**, and a pilot customer impresses investors more than any slide.

**Step 1 — pilots before money (first 1–3 months):**
- Get 2–3 companies to try it free or cheap for a month. What you want from them isn't revenue — it's **proof**: "Firm X captured 500 pieces of knowledge in 30 days and reused 40% of them."
- Investors fund evidence, not ideas.

**Step 2 — pre-seed funding (roughly the first 6 months):**
- Sources, in rough order of ease: hackathon/ecosystem prizes → government startup grants (Singapore and Vietnam both have generous ones for AI startups) → **accelerators** (they give a small amount of money + mentorship + investor introductions in exchange for a small ownership slice) → **angel investors** (wealthy individuals, often ex-founders) → early-stage venture funds like GenAI Fund itself.
- Typical pre-seed in this region: enough to pay a small team for about a year. You give away roughly 10–20% ownership. Don't give away more this early.

**Step 3 — seed round (once pilots convert to paying customers):**
- With a handful of paying companies and a growth story, raise a proper seed round from venture funds to hire and sell faster.

**What investors will ask — have answers ready:**
- *"Why won't the big AI companies just build this?"* → They build general tools for everyone. We build the **organization's** memory with **leadership steering** and full traceability — a company-first design, not a chatbot feature. And whoever holds a company's memory first is very hard to displace.
- *"How is this different from a shared drive or a wiki?"* → Wikis rot because humans must maintain them and nobody does. Our memory **maintains itself** — that's the entire point.
- *"What stops knowledge from being wrong?"* → The Auditor checks everything against company rules, everything is traceable to its source, and humans can see and challenge every decision. Trust is a feature, not a hope.

## 8. Competition (know the landscape, don't fear it)

- **The inspiration:** Garry Tan (a famous Silicon Valley investor) publicly described "G-brain," an internal tool that turns his firm's knowledge into a connected map. That's validation that smart money believes in this category. **Our twist:** his idea maps knowledge; ours adds AI librarians that *maintain* the map and take direction from leadership's own memos. Cite him as proof of the trend, then state the difference.
- **Big workplace tools** (document wikis, company search engines): great at storing and finding files, but they don't clean themselves, don't capture the *why*, and don't enforce company rules automatically.
- **Note-taking AI tools:** capture individual conversations but build personal memory, not company memory with governance.

One-liner: **"Everyone else stores knowledge. We keep it alive."**

## 9. Words to use / words to avoid on stage

Use these (simple, vivid):
- "company memory" · "knowledge walks out the door" · "AI librarians" · "the memory maintains itself" · "leadership steers by writing memos" · "nothing is a black box" · "every trip around the loop makes the company smarter"

If a judge asks a technical question, hand it to the tech team with a smile: "Great question — [name] built exactly that part."
